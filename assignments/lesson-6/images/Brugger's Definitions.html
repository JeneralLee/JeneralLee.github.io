Brugger's Definition

Neoclassic vs. Romantic

Neoclassic Period – 1660 to 1800

Romantic Period – 1789 (French Revolution) or 1798 (Lyrical Ballads) to 1830-35

Definitions difficult: either too vague, meaningless, or too specific; ignore range & variety.

  • Neoclassic authors: Dryden, Pope, Addison, Swift, Johnson, Burke, Gray.
  • strong traditionalism & distrust of radical innovation
  • respect for classical writers (ancient Greece & Rome), who were thought to have achieved excellence & established enduring models in all genres
  • literature was primarily an art, a set of skills perfected by long study & practice
  • literature of the time was a deliberate adaptation from classical models
  • “Neoclassic ideal” was the craftsman’s ideal, demanding polish, careful & extensive revision, stylistic decorum, attention to detail
  • natural genius was a thing of the past
  • human beings were the primary subject matter of literature
  • poetry was an imitation of human life, “a mirror held up to nature”
  • poetry yielded both instruction & pleasure to its readers
  • not “art for art’s sake,” but “art for humanity’s sake”
  • Emphasis on representative human characteristics, shared experiences, etc.
  • many works attacked human pride, presumptuousness, extreme behavior
  • Romantic authors: Wordsworth, Coleridge, Burns, Keats, Byron, Shelley, Austen.
  • favored innovation, even revolution, in literature
  • common life, lowly subjects, & natural language exalted as ideal
  • explorations into the supernatural were common
  • notion of “poet-as-prophet” emerged, along with genre of visionary poetry
  • symbolism became more important; objects charged with a significance beyond their physical qualities
  • “spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings recollected in tranquility”
  • poet’s own feelings—not rules or decorum—matter
  • composition is not artful manipulation, but the unforced product of deep reflection & meditation; it’s organic & inherently imaginative
  • nature—landscape, flora, fauna—became the subject of poetry (but not “nature poets”)
  • poetry invited its readers to identify with protagonists, even the poets themselves
  • little emphasis on organized society; protagonists were often solitary figures, outcasts, rebels, or nonconformists engaged in some elusive quest
  • age of new beginnings, high possibilities; humans endowed with limitless aspirations
  • highest art is an endeavor beyond finite human possibility; artist’s failure attests to the grandeur of his aim
